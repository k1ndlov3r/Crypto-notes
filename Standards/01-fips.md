# Federal Information Processing Standards (FIPS)

## Overview

Federal Information Processing Standards (FIPS) are publicly announced standards developed by the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) for use by U.S. federal government agencies and contractors.

These standards define requirements for cryptographic algorithms, security modules, identity verification, and other information security practices.

---

## Objectives

- Standardize security requirements across federal agencies.
- Ensure interoperability between government systems.
- Improve the security of information processing.
- Define approved cryptographic algorithms.

---

## Common FIPS Standards

### FIPS 140

**Security Requirements for Cryptographic Modules**

Defines four security levels for cryptographic hardware and software modules.

Examples include:

- Hardware Security Modules (HSMs)
- Smart cards
- Encryption libraries

---

### FIPS 186

**Digital Signature Standard (DSS)**

Defines approved digital signature algorithms, including:

- RSA
- DSA
- ECDSA

---

### FIPS 197

**Advanced Encryption Standard (AES)**

Official specification for AES.

Key sizes:

- AES-128
- AES-192
- AES-256

AES replaced the older Data Encryption Standard (DES).

---

### FIPS 201

**Personal Identity Verification (PIV)**

Defines secure identity credentials for U.S. federal employees and contractors.

---

## Why FIPS Matters

Many organizations require FIPS-compliant cryptography, especially:

- Government agencies
- Defense contractors
- Healthcare
- Financial institutions

Many cryptographic products advertise **FIPS 140 validation** as proof of compliance.

---

## Summary

FIPS standards define approved security requirements and cryptographic algorithms used by the U.S. federal government. They provide a trusted baseline for implementing secure systems.
